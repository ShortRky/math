Continuity

Chapter 2: Limits and Continuity Lesson 5 of 6 ⏱️ 30-40 minutes

🎯 Goal

Understand what it means for a function to be continuous and how to check for continuity.

In one sentence: A function is continuous if you can draw it without lifting your pencil.

💡 The Idea

Definition of Continuity

f is continuous at x = a if:

  1. f(a) is defined
  2. lim(x→a) f(x) exists
  3. lim(x→a) f(x) = f(a)

Types of Discontinuity

Removable

A hole in the graph — limit exists but f(a) is different or undefined

Jump

Left and right limits exist but are different

Infinite

Function goes to infinity — vertical asymptote

Why This Matters

Continuity is essential for calculus. Many theorems (like the Intermediate Value Theorem) only work for continuous functions.

📝 Example

Example: Is f(x) = (x² - 1)/(x - 1) continuous at x = 1?

Step 1: Check f(1)

f(1) = (1-1)/(1-1) = 0/0 (undefined)

Step 2: Check limit

lim(x→1) (x²-1)/(x-1) = lim(x→1) (x+1) = 2

Step 3: Compare

f(1) undefined, so not continuous at x = 1

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Checking Continuity

  1. Check if f(a) exists — Is the function defined?
  2. Find the limit — Does lim(x→a) f(x) exist?
  3. Compare — Is the limit equal to f(a)?
  4. State conclusion — Continuous or not?

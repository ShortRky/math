The Mean Value Theorem

Chapter 4: Applications of Derivatives Lesson 4 of 6 ⏱️ 30-40 minutes

🎯 Goal

Understand the Mean Value Theorem and its applications.

In one sentence: The Mean Value Theorem says that at some point, the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change.

💡 The Idea

The Mean Value Theorem

If f is continuous on [a, b] and differentiable on (a, b), then there exists at least one c in (a, b) such that:

f'(c) = [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a)

What It Means

At some point, the tangent line is parallel to the secant line connecting the endpoints.

Why This Matters

The Mean Value Theorem is a fundamental result that connects average and instantaneous rates of change. It's used to prove many other theorems.

📝 Example

Example: Find c for f(x) = x² on [1, 3]

Step 1: Find average rate

[f(3) - f(1)]/(3-1) = (9-1)/2 = 4

Step 2: Find f'(x)

f'(x) = 2x

Step 3: Set equal and solve

2c = 4, so c = 2

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Applying the Mean Value Theorem

  1. Check conditions — Is f continuous and differentiable?
  2. Find average rate — [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a)
  3. Find f'(x) — Differentiate
  4. Set equal — f'(c) = average rate
  5. Solve for c — Find the value(s)

The Chain Rule

Chapter 3: Derivatives Lesson 7 of 7 ⏱️ 35-45 minutes

🎯 Goal

Learn how to differentiate composite functions (functions inside other functions).

In one sentence: The chain rule says d/dx [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))·g'(x).

💡 The Idea

The Chain Rule

d/dx [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))·g'(x)

"Derivative of the outside, keep the inside, times derivative of the inside"

When to Use It

Use the chain rule when you have a function inside another function, like:

  • (x² + 1)³
  • sin(x³)
  • e^(2x)
  • √(x + 5)

Why This Matters

The chain rule is one of the most important rules in calculus. It's used constantly in physics, engineering, and advanced mathematics.

📝 Example

Example: Find d/dx [(x² + 1)³]

Step 1: Identify outer and inner

Outer: f(u) = u³, Inner: g(x) = x² + 1

Step 2: Find derivatives

f'(u) = 3u², g'(x) = 2x

Step 3: Apply chain rule

d/dx [(x²+1)³] = 3(x²+1)²·2x = 6x(x²+1)²

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Using the Chain Rule

  1. Identify outer and inner functions — What's inside what?
  2. Find derivative of outer — Keep the inner
  3. Find derivative of inner — Differentiate inside
  4. Multiply them — Outer'·Inner'
  5. Simplify — Clean up the expression

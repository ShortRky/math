Trigonometric Substitution

Chapter 1: Integration Techniques Lesson 3 of 6 ⏱️ 35-45 minutes

🎯 Goal

Learn how to use trigonometric substitution to evaluate integrals involving square roots.

In one sentence: Trigonometric substitution uses trig identities to simplify integrals with square roots of quadratic expressions.

💡 The Idea

When to Use Trigonometric Substitution

Use when you see:

  • √(a² - x²) — use x = a·sin(θ)
  • √(a² + x²) — use x = a·tan(θ)
  • √(x² - a²) — use x = a·sec(θ)

Why It Works

Trigonometric identities simplify the square roots:

1 - sin²(θ) = cos²(θ)
1 + tan²(θ) = sec²(θ)
sec²(θ) - 1 = tan²(θ)

Why This Matters

Trigonometric substitution is essential for integrals that appear in physics and engineering, especially those involving circular or elliptical motion.

📝 Example

Example: Find ∫√(4 - x²)dx

Step 1: Choose substitution

Let x = 2·sin(θ), then dx = 2·cos(θ)dθ

Step 2: Simplify

√(4 - x²) = √(4 - 4sin²(θ)) = 2cos(θ)

Step 3: Integrate

∫2cos(θ)·2cos(θ)dθ = 4∫cos²(θ)dθ = 2θ + sin(2θ) + C

Step 4: Substitute back

θ = arcsin(x/2), so answer involves arcsin(x/2)

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Trigonometric Substitution

  1. Identify the form — Which square root do you have?
  2. Choose substitution — sin, tan, or sec
  3. Find dx — Differentiate your substitution
  4. Simplify — Use trig identities
  5. Integrate — Evaluate the trig integral
  6. Substitute back — Return to original variable

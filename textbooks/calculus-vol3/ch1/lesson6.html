Cylindrical and Spherical Coordinates

Chapter 1: Vectors and the Geometry of Space Lesson 6 of 6 ⏱️ 40-50 minutes

🎯 Goal

Learn how to use cylindrical and spherical coordinates to describe points in 3D space.

In one sentence: Cylindrical and spherical coordinates provide alternative ways to describe 3D space that are often more convenient than Cartesian coordinates.

💡 The Idea

Why Use Different Coordinates?

Some problems are easier to solve in different coordinate systems. For example:

  • Cylindrical coordinates are great for problems with cylindrical symmetry
  • Spherical coordinates are great for problems with spherical symmetry

Cylindrical Coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates use (r, θ, z) where:

  • r = distance from the z-axis (like polar coordinates)
  • θ = angle in the xy-plane from the positive x-axis
  • z = height (same as Cartesian z)

Conversion Formulas

From cylindrical to Cartesian:

x = r cos(θ)
y = r sin(θ)
z = z

From Cartesian to cylindrical:

r = √(x² + y²)
θ = arctan(y/x)
z = z

Spherical Coordinates

Spherical coordinates use (ρ, θ, φ) where:

  • ρ = distance from the origin
  • θ = angle in the xy-plane from the positive x-axis (same as cylindrical)
  • φ = angle from the positive z-axis (polar angle)

Conversion Formulas

From spherical to Cartesian:

x = ρ sin(φ) cos(θ)
y = ρ sin(φ) sin(θ)
z = ρ cos(φ)

From Cartesian to spherical:

ρ = √(x² + y² + z²)
θ = arctan(y/x)
φ = arccos(z/ρ)

Common Surfaces

In Cylindrical Coordinates

  • r = constant → cylinder
  • θ = constant → half-plane
  • z = constant → plane

In Spherical Coordinates

  • ρ = constant → sphere
  • θ = constant → half-plane
  • φ = constant → cone

Why This Matters

Choosing the right coordinate system can make problems much easier to solve. This is essential in physics, engineering, and computer graphics where symmetry plays a key role.

📝 Example

Example 1: Converting from Cartesian to Cylindrical

Problem: Convert (3, 4, 5) to cylindrical coordinates

Step 1: Find r

r = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

Step 2: Find θ

θ = arctan(4/3) ≈ 53.13°

Step 3: Keep z

z = 5

Step 4: Write the answer

(r, θ, z) = (5, 53.13°, 5)

Example 2: Converting from Cartesian to Spherical

Problem: Convert (1, 1, √2) to spherical coordinates

Step 1: Find ρ

ρ = √(1² + 1² + (√2)²) = √(1 + 1 + 2) = √4 = 2

Step 2: Find θ

θ = arctan(1/1) = 45°

Step 3: Find φ

φ = arccos(√2/2) = 45°

Step 4: Write the answer

(ρ, θ, φ) = (2, 45°, 45°)

Example 3: Identifying a Surface

Problem: Describe the surface r = 3 in cylindrical coordinates

Step 1: Understand what r = 3 means

This means the distance from the z-axis is always 3.

Step 2: Visualize

This is a cylinder of radius 3 centered on the z-axis.

Step 3: Write in Cartesian

x² + y² = 9

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Converting Coordinates

  1. Identify the system — What are you converting from and to?
  2. Use the formulas — Apply the appropriate conversion formulas
  3. Calculate each component — Find r, θ, z or ρ, θ, φ
  4. Check your work — Convert back to verify
  5. Write the answer — Use the correct notation

Step-by-Step: Identifying Surfaces

  1. Look at the equation — Which coordinate is constant?
  2. Think geometrically — What shape does this describe?
  3. Visualize — Picture the surface in 3D
  4. Convert if needed — Write in Cartesian for clarity

Double Integrals in Polar

Chapter 3: Multiple Integrals Lesson 2 of 6 ⏱️ 35-45 minutes

🎯 Goal

Learn how to evaluate double integrals using polar coordinates, which simplifies integration over circular regions.

In one sentence: Polar coordinates make it easier to integrate over circular regions by using r and θ instead of x and y.

💡 The Idea

Polar Coordinates

Polar coordinates use r (distance from origin) and θ (angle from positive x-axis) instead of x and y:

x = r cos(θ)
y = r sin(θ)
r² = x² + y²

Area Element in Polar

The area element dA in polar coordinates is:

dA = r dr dθ

The extra factor of r comes from the Jacobian of the transformation.

Double Integral in Polar

The double integral of f(x, y) over a region R becomes:

∬ᴿ f(x, y) dA = ∬ᴿ f(r cos(θ), r sin(θ)) r dr dθ

When to Use Polar

  • Circular regions — disks, annuli, sectors
  • Integrands with x² + y² — becomes r²
  • Symmetry about origin — simplifies bounds

Why This Matters

Polar coordinates are essential for solving integrals over circular regions that would be very difficult or impossible in Cartesian coordinates. They're widely used in physics, engineering, and probability.

📝 Example

Example 1: Integral over a Disk

Problem: Evaluate ∬ᴿ (x² + y²) dA where R is the disk x² + y² ≤ 4

Step 1: Convert to polar

x² + y² = r²
dA = r dr dθ
Bounds: 0 ≤ r ≤ 2, 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π

Step 2: Set up the integral

∫₀²π ∫₀² r² · r dr dθ = ∫₀²π ∫₀² r³ dr dθ

Step 3: Evaluate the inner integral

∫₀² r³ dr = [r⁴/4]₀² = 16/4 = 4

Step 4: Evaluate the outer integral

∫₀²π 4 dθ = 4θ |₀²π = 8π

Example 2: Integral over a Sector

Problem: Evaluate ∬ᴿ xy dA where R is the region in the first quadrant bounded by x² + y² = 1

Step 1: Convert to polar

xy = r cos(θ) · r sin(θ) = r² cos(θ) sin(θ)
Bounds: 0 ≤ r ≤ 1, 0 ≤ θ ≤ π/2

Step 2: Set up the integral

∫₀π/² ∫₀¹ r² cos(θ) sin(θ) · r dr dθ = ∫₀π/² ∫₀¹ r³ cos(θ) sin(θ) dr dθ

Step 3: Evaluate the inner integral

∫₀¹ r³ dr = [r⁴/4]₀¹ = 1/4

Step 4: Evaluate the outer integral

∫₀π/² (1/4) cos(θ) sin(θ) dθ = (1/4) ∫₀π/² sin(θ) cos(θ) dθ
= (1/4) [sin²(θ)/2]₀π/² = (1/4)(1/2) = 1/8

📋 How to Solve Most Questions

Step-by-Step: Converting to Polar

  1. Identify the region — Is it circular or sector-shaped?
  2. Convert the integrand — Replace x² + y² with r², x with r cos(θ), y with r sin(θ)
  3. Include the Jacobian — Multiply by r
  4. Find the bounds — Determine r and θ limits
  5. Evaluate — Integrate with respect to r first, then θ
